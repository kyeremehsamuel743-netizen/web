History of Bugatti

Bugatti's history dates back to its founding in 1909 by Ettore Bugatti in Molsheim, Alsace. Ettore was a visionary industrial designer who combined artistic elegance with engineering innovation. The early years saw Bugatti rapidly build a reputation for high-performance racing cars, starting production in 1910. Bugatti's Type 13 was successful in Grand Prix racing, contributing to the brand's prestige. Alongside cars, Bugatti designed airplane engines during WWI which helped finance the business. The company produced luxury and racing vehicles, as well as railcars, but faced financial and labor difficulties in the 1930s. After WWII, Bugatti struggled and formally ceased production in 1956, largely due to financial challenges and the death of key figures. The brand was revived in the late 1980s under the ownership of Italian Romano Artioli, who produced the EB110 supercar from 1991 to 1995. In 1998, Volkswagen Group acquired Bugatti and established Bugatti Automobiles S.A.S. headquartered once again in Molsheim. Under VW, Bugatti has become known for hypercars like the Veyron and Chiron, setting records in speed, engineering, and luxury. The history of Bugatti spans over a century from artisanal racing roots to modern ultra-luxury and performance engineering, marked by prestige, innovation, and resilience throughout its phases of growth, decline, and rebirth.
